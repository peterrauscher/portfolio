When I graduated, the debate was always the same: big tech stability vs. early startup risk. Google or the scrappy 10-person team in a WeWork. Most of my classmates picked one extreme or the other. Both are wrong.

The best first job in tech is a mid-stage startup - Series B or C, somewhere between 50 and 500 people, with a product that's already working.

Here's why.

**Your work will actually ship.** At a big company, you're a cog in a machine that was running long before you arrived. You'll spend your first year in onboarding, getting permissions, and learning internal tooling no one outside the company has ever heard of. At a tiny startup, you'll spend it firefighting existential crises with no resources. A mid-stage company is past the "will this survive?" phase but nowhere near the "protect the moat" phase. You build something, it ships, real users touch it. The feedback loop is fast and that's where you learn.

**Growth creates titles faster than tenure does.** These companies are spinning up new teams constantly. If you're good, you'll be leading one within a year or two - not because you waited long enough, but because there's no one else. That's not a gap in leadership, that's your opportunity. Take it.

**The network compounds.** The founders and early team are still in the building. You're not working for some VP who reports to a director who reports to a C-suite you'll never meet. You're sitting near the people who built the thing from scratch, and they will become your professional network for the next 20 years - future employers, future cofounders, future investors. No LinkedIn connection request compares to actually building something together.

**The equity math is better than you think.** Seed-stage options are lottery tickets with bad odds. FAANG RSUs have no meaningful upside. A Series B company that's already proven product-market fit and is on a clear path to IPO or acquisition in 2-3 years? That's the sweet spot. You're not taking a flyer, you're buying in after the concept risk is gone but before the market has priced you out.

If you're a new grad in tech, resist the pull of the obvious choices. The FAANG offer feels prestigious and the tiny startup feels exciting. But the place where you'll grow fastest, matter most, and build the best foundation - is the company that's already found its footing and is now sprinting.

That's where the real leverage is.
